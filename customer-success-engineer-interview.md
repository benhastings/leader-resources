# Customer Success Engineering — Interview Questions

## Foundation - Technical Background, Learning Style, Collaboration

- Tell us about your background with technology
- What are 1-2 things that you're looking forward to in the world of technology?

- Tell us about a time (work or otherwise) when you had to solve a completely new to you problem and what you did to arrive at the solution

- When friends and team members are asked to describe you, what are the words or phrases that they most commonly use?
- What do you consider to be your greatest strengths when working under high-pressure situations?
- What are you greatest growth areas that you're currently pursuing?

## Technical Problem-Solving

- Walk me through how you'd debug a customer's integration that was working yesterday and isn't today. What's your process before you touch anything?
- A customer says "your API is slow." What do you actually need to know before you can help them?
- Tell me about a time you had to get deeply technical in a domain you weren't already expert in to solve a customer problem. How did you approach it and what was the result?
- Have you ever identified a bug or architectural issue in your own product while helping a customer? What did you do with that information?

> What to listen for: generically vs specific incidents. Systematic thinking, intellectual curiosity, and closed loops — they didn't just fix it, they followed it through.

## Customer Empathy & Communication
- How do you adjust your technical communication when you're talking to an engineer vs. a VP vs. a skeptical end user?
- Give me an example of a customer who was frustrated or losing confidence in the product. What did you do?
- Have you ever had to tell a customer something they didn't want to hear — a limitation, a delayed fix, a "no"? Walk me through how you handled it.
- Describe a customer who was technically wrong about why something wasn't working. How did you correct them without damaging the relationship?

> What to listen for: Great candidates protect the customer relationship and tell the truth. Mediocre ones either capitulate to the customer or prioritize being right. Watch for candidates who stay curious rather than defensive when a customer pushes back.

## Handling Ambiguity & Escalations
- You've got three customers waiting on you, one of them is escalating loudly, and your engineering contact is out for the day. How do you triage?
- What's your threshold for escalating something internally vs. continuing to work it yourself?
- Tell me about the messiest escalation you've been part of. What made it hard, and what would you do differently?
- Describe a situation where you had no clear owner for a customer problem — it wasn't quite support, wasn't quite engineering, wasn't quite PM. What did you do?

> What to listen for: Great candidates don't just manage the situation — they change the conditions that caused it. They create clarity, set up better processes, and make the next escalation easier. Mediocre ones describe surviving the incident without any reflection on systemic improvement.

## Cross-Functional Collaboration

- How do you typically work with product or engineering when a customer surfaces a bug or a missing capability?
- How do you make sure customer pain actually influences the product roadmap?
- Tell me about a time you disagreed with how engineering or PM prioritized a customer issue. What did you do?
- Have you ever had to represent multiple competing customers to an internal team at once? How did you frame the tradeoffs?

> What to listen for: Mediocre candidates describe themselves as messengers — they relay feedback and wait. Good candidates advocate. Great candidates show up with data, frame business impact, and build internal credibility so their voice carries weight over time.

## Onboarding & Post-Sales Adoption

- What does a successful onboarding look like to you, and how do you know when a customer is actually set up for success vs. just technically deployed?
- How do you handle a customer who's been live for six months but is barely using the product?
- Describe a customer implementation that went sideways. What were the early signals and how did you respond?
- How have you approached expansion or upsell conversations without it feeling like a sales call?


> What to listen for: The best candidates understand that adoption is behavioral, not just technical. They proactively monitor leading indicators of churn or stagnation — they don't wait for a renewal call to find out the customer is unhappy.
